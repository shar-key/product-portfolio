# Market Research Framework

Product decisions made without verified research are guesses with deadlines. 
This framework describes how I structure market research to produce 
investor-grade, decision-ready evidence.

## The Validated Research Brief

Every product or market I work in gets a Validated Research Brief before any 
strategy is finalized. The brief separates verified facts from working 
hypotheses and ensures no investor claim or product decision rests on 
unverified data.

## Research Structure

**Problem Landscape**
What is broken in the market and for whom. Quantified where possible. 
Every statistic traced to an independently verifiable source.

**Market Sizing**
Total Addressable Market, Serviceable Addressable Market, and initial 
target segment. Methodology documented. Conservative estimates preferred 
over optimistic ones.

**Competitive Analysis**
Who else is solving this problem and how. White space identification. 
Positioning gaps that create an entry point.

**Customer Evidence**
Primary research findings from discovery interviews. Quoted language 
from real users. Behavioral patterns that validate or invalidate the 
core hypothesis.

**Verified Source Standards**
Every metric in a Validated Research Brief must meet one of these 
criteria: published by an independent research firm, sourced from 
primary legal or regulatory documents, or confirmed by multiple 
independent sources. Internal data is labeled as hypothesis until 
externally validated.

## What Gets Flagged

Any claim that cannot be traced to a verified source is explicitly 
labeled as an unvalidated hypothesis. This protects against 
overconfident roadmaps and investor claims that cannot withstand 
diligence.

## Research Sources I Trust

SHRM for HR industry benchmarks. Crosschq for quality of hire data. 
Bessemer Venture Partners and OpenView for SaaS benchmarks. Primary 
legal sources for compliance requirements. Peer-reviewed research for 
behavioral and psychological claims.

## What I Do Not Do

Cite internal company projections as market evidence. Use a single 
source for any critical claim. Present optimistic TAM figures without 
a documented methodology. Treat analyst reports as ground truth without 
cross-referencing primary sources.
